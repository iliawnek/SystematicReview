What is systematic review?

A systematic review is a type of literature review that collects and critically analyzes multiple research studies or papers. A review of existing studies is often quicker and cheaper than embarking on a new study. Researchers use methods that are selected before one or more research questions are formulated, and then they aim to find and analyze studies that relate to and answer those questions. Systematic reviews of randomized controlled trials are key in the practice of evidence-based medicine.

What is the systematic review process?

The review organises papers into 4 pools:

  • Abstract pool
  • Document pool
  • Final pool
  • Rejected pool

All papers returned by the query are first stored in the abstract pool. Systematic review takes you through each paper in the abstract pool, and shows you the paper's title and abstract. From this, you can decide on whether the paper is appropriate for your particular needs.

Every paper you approve from the abstract pool gets moved into the document pool. The document pool is much like the abstract pool, but this time you are given a link to view the paper in full via PubMed. Again, you can decide on whether or not the paper is relevant.

After deciding a paper is relevant in the document pool, it gets moved into the final pool, which is a list of all papers relevant to your review! Any irrelevant papers are moved into the rejected pool. Papers in the final pool can be rejected if later found to be irrelevant. Likewise, papers in the rejected pool can be reconsidered by moving them back into the abstract or document pool.

How do I use this web app?

Start off by creating a new review. Choose a title and write a description for your new review. Here, you can add the usernames or email addresses of other users who you would like to invite to join you in the review.

Next, write a query to search for medical papers related to your review on PubMed. Keep adjusting your query until it returns under 500 papers.

Once your review is created, you can work on the review, which automatically presents a random paper from the abstract pool or document pool. Papers in the abstract pool get priority, so you will always be presented with a paper from the abstract pool if one exists.

After judging all papers presented, the review will be marked as completed! You will be able to view a list of all papers in the final pool, visit links to each paper on PubMed, and download the results as a text file.

At any point during the review, you can rename it, add or remove participants, modify the query, or delete it.

How do I write a query?

Write down each keyword separated by AND and OR. Use brackets () to group parts of the query.

For example:

(adhd OR adhs OR addh) AND (child OR adolescent) AND acupuncture

Every paper returned by this query must contain acupuncture. It must also contain either child, or adolescent, or both. And finally, a returned paper must also contain some variant of adhd.

As a general guideline, adding more ANDs will return fewer papers, but adding more ORs will return more papers.
